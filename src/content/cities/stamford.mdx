## How to Choose a Legal Nurse Consultant in Stamford

- **Verify credentials before the conversation goes further.** LNCC (from AALNC) and CLNC (Vickie Milazzo Institute) are the two major designations — they're not the same, and neither is automatically better, but an LNC who can't name their credential body is a red flag. For life care plan work specifically, look for CNLCP.
- **Match their clinical background to your case type.** A nurse who spent 20 years in orthopedic surgery is going to miss things in a neurology malpractice case that an ICU nurse would catch immediately. Ask for a CV with specific unit history, not just "hospital experience."
- **Ask whether they've testified in Connecticut courts.** Connecticut's expert witness standards and the nuances of Superior Court in Stamford differ from federal practice. An LNC who's been deposed in Bridgeport is worth more on a Fairfield County case than one who's only done paper review in another state.
- **Get a sample work product.** A medical record chronology or causation memo tells you more in five minutes than a 30-minute phone screen. Any experienced LNC should have a redacted sample ready.
- **Confirm their bandwidth before you commit.** Stamford attracts complex, document-heavy matters — securities fraud with physical injury claims, product liability cases from the pharma corridor up I-95. An LNC juggling 12 active files may not have the bandwidth for a 40,000-page medical record review.

> **Pro Tip:** Connecticut doesn't require LNC licensure beyond RN status, which means the market is unregulated. The credentialing bodies (AALNC, Vickie Milazzo) are the only quality signal you have. Always verify certification directly at the issuing body's website — not just from the consultant's bio.

## What to Expect

Most LNC engagements in Stamford run $2,000–$10,000 per case, with straightforward record reviews on the lower end and full litigation support packages (expert witness screening, deposition prep, trial support) pushing toward the top of that range. Initial case screening typically runs 2–4 days; full causation analysis on complex matters can take 3–6 weeks depending on record volume.

> **Reality Check:** The single most common pricing mistake attorneys make is hiring for the cheapest initial review, then re-hiring when the case gets complicated. An LNC who quotes $1,500 for a "quick look" and then bills hourly for every follow-up question will cost you more than one who quoted $4,500 flat for a comprehensive opinion. Ask for a scope-of-work in writing before any engagement starts.
