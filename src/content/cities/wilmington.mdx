## How to Choose a Legal Nurse Consultant in Wilmington

- **Verify credentials before the first call.** The LNCC (issued by AALNC) and CLNC (Vickie Milazzo Institute) are the two credentials that actually signal training in legal work — not just clinical hours. Ask which they hold and when they were last renewed.
- **Match specialty to case type.** A nurse with 15 years in orthopedic surgery is the wrong call for a neonatal malpractice matter. Delaware's court docket skews toward corporate litigation, but the New Castle County Superior Court sees a steady mix of personal injury and workers' comp — get someone who's reviewed similar records, not just similar volume.
- **Ask for a redacted work sample.** Any experienced LNC will have sanitized examples of a standard of care analysis or a chronology. If they hesitate, that's your answer.
- **Check for life care planning experience if damages are contested.** If your case involves catastrophic injury, a consultant with CNLCP or MSCC credentials can build a certified life care plan that holds up to Daubert scrutiny — a separate skill from records review.
- **Confirm turnaround capacity upfront.** Wilmington is a smaller market; some consultants juggle multiple firms and Philadelphia overflow simultaneously. Get a written estimate of availability before you're staring down a disclosure deadline.

> **Pro Tip:** Delaware attorneys have access to the AALNC member directory, which lets you filter by state and specialty. Cross-reference any candidate's name there before you commit — it takes two minutes and weeds out the self-credentialed.

## What to Expect

A standard case engagement in Wilmington runs $2,000–$10,000 depending on record volume, complexity, and whether you need a full standard of care opinion or just a screening review to decide whether a case is worth pursuing. Expect an initial consult of one to three hours, a written chronology and issues memo within two to four weeks, and a separate fee structure if you need the LNC to testify or attend depositions.

> **Reality Check:** The biggest pricing mistake attorneys make is scoping only the records review and then going back for three rounds of addenda as new records surface. Lock in an hourly overage rate at the start — not a flat project fee with a vague "additional records may incur extra charges" clause — so you're not negotiating mid-case.
