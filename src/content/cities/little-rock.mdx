## How to Choose a Legal Nurse Consultant in Little Rock

- **Verify the credential, not just the title.** LNCC (from AALNC) and CLNC (Vickie Milazzo Institute) are the two recognized certifications — but they're not equivalent. LNCC requires active RN licensure plus two years of LNC practice before you can even sit for the exam. Ask which cert they hold and when they last renewed it.
- **Match clinical background to case type.** A critical care nurse reviewing a surgical negligence claim is a better fit than a pediatric nurse reviewing the same file. For the UAMS-adjacent cases that show up frequently in Little Rock, ask specifically about their hospital-side experience.
- **Ask for a sample case summary.** Any experienced LNC can show you a redacted work product. If they hesitate, that tells you something. You're looking for clear timeline reconstruction, specific standard-of-care citations, and citations to medical literature — not just a narrative retelling of the chart.
- **Confirm their Arkansas-specific familiarity.** Arkansas has its own medical review panel process and statute of limitations quirks. An LNC who primarily works Texas or Tennessee cases will have a learning curve on local procedural nuances.
- **Check turnaround commitments in writing.** Verbal promises about two-week delivery mean nothing when you're three weeks from a deposition. Get scope, deliverables, and timeline in the engagement letter.

> **Pro Tip:** For workers' comp cases in Arkansas, ask if the LNC also holds MSCC (Medicare Set-Aside Certified Consultant) credentials. With CMS's expanding MSA review requirements, having that expertise in-house on the same file saves a second engagement fee.

## What to Expect

LNC engagements in Little Rock typically run $2,000–$10,000 per case, with most plaintiff-side medical malpractice files landing in the $3,500–$6,000 range for initial records review, a standard-of-care analysis, and a written report. Life care plan development sits at the higher end and often runs separately. Turnaround on a focused records review is usually 2–3 weeks; complex multi-defendant cases can run 6–8 weeks.

> **Reality Check:** The single most common billing mistake attorneys make is treating the initial consultation fee as the full engagement cost. It's not. Deposition prep, expert witness screening, and trial support are typically billed separately — often at hourly rates between $150–$350/hr. Get a full scope-of-work estimate before you sign the engagement letter, not after.
