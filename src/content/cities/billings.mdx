## How to Choose a Legal Nurse Consultant in Billings

- **Verify the credential, not just the title.** LNCC (from AALNC) and CLNC (from the Vickie Milazzo Institute) are the two recognized marks. Anyone calling themselves a "legal nurse consultant" without one of those behind their name is self-titled. Ask for the certificate number and verify it directly with the issuing body.
- **Match the specialty to the case type.** A trauma ICU nurse reviewing a wound-care negligence claim is the wrong fit. Montana cases skew toward agricultural injuries, oilfield accidents (especially Bakken-adjacent work), and rural hospital delays in transfer. Look for someone with relevant clinical floor time, not just courtroom exposure.
- **Ask specifically about Montana standard-of-care familiarity.** Critical access hospitals operate under different staffing ratios and transfer protocols than urban Level I trauma centers. An LNC who only knows large metro systems will miss the nuance that wins or loses a rural negligence case.
- **Get a sample work product before you commit.** A credible LNC will have a redacted chronology or screening memo they can share. If they can't produce one, that tells you something.
- **Confirm life care planning credentials separately if you need them.** CNLCP (Certified Nurse Life Care Planner) is a distinct credential from LNCC/CLNC. Don't assume a litigation-support LNC can also quantify long-term care costs for a damages calculation — those are different skill sets.

> **Pro Tip:** Billings is the regional hub for eastern Montana and parts of Wyoming and the Dakotas. A well-networked LNC here likely has relationships with Billings Clinic and St. Vincent Healthcare staff — which matters when you need a treating provider screened as a potential expert witness on short notice.

## What to Expect

Standard case engagements run $2,000–$10,000 depending on complexity, record volume, and whether you need a full chronology, expert witness screening, or both. Hourly rates typically fall between $150–$300/hour for experienced credentialed consultants. Turnaround on an initial screening memo is usually 5–10 business days; full chronologies on large-record cases can run 3–4 weeks.

> **Reality Check:** The most common billing mistake attorneys make is confusing a flat-fee quote with a capped engagement. Most LNCs quote a flat fee for defined scope — if the records expand mid-case (and they always do), expect a change order. Get the scope nailed down in writing before authorizing work, or that $3,500 estimate becomes $7,000 before the first deposition.
