## How to Choose a Legal Nurse Consultant in Tucson

- **Verify the credential, not just the title.** LNCC (awarded by AALNC) and CLNC (Vickie Milazzo Institute) are not the same thing and carry different weight in federal vs. state court. Know which one your matter calls for before you pick up the phone.
- **Match specialty to case type.** A trauma ICU nurse is not your first call on a long-term care negligence case. Tucson's Banner-University Medical Center and Tucson Medical Center handle a high volume of complex trauma — if your case involves emergency or surgical care, prioritize LNCs with acute care backgrounds.
- **Ask for a case list in your jurisdiction.** Arizona follows a modified comparative fault standard, and LNCs who've worked Pima County Superior Court cases understand what plaintiff and defense counsel here actually need in a standard-of-care analysis.
- **Clarify deliverable format upfront.** Some LNCs produce a narrative chronology; others deliver annotated record summaries, gap analyses, or litigation support binders. Mismatched expectations are how you blow your budget on the wrong document.
- **Check for life care planning credentials separately.** If your matter involves long-term damages — spinal cord injury, TBI, catastrophic burn — you need a CNLCP or MSCC, not just a general LNC. These are distinct credentialing tracks.

> **Pro Tip:** Arizona has a robust University of Arizona Medical Center ecosystem, which means Tucson-area LNCs often have deep exposure to academic medicine and clinical research settings — useful if your case involves an experimental treatment or off-label drug protocol.

## What to Expect

LNC engagements in Tucson typically run $2,000–$10,000 per case, with hourly rates ranging from $150–$350 depending on credential level, specialty, and whether you need testifying expert support or purely consulting work. Most initial record reviews turn around in 7–14 business days; rush turnarounds are available but priced accordingly.

> **Reality Check:** The most common billing mistake is treating LNC hours like paralegal hours. Attorneys who try to negotiate LNC rates to $75/hour based on "comparable" legal support work end up with one of two outcomes — they don't get the experienced consultant they needed, or they get someone who front-loads hours to compensate. Pay market rate, define scope tightly, and you'll come out ahead on both cost and quality.
