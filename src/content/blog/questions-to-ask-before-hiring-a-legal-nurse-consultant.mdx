The first time my colleague handed a case file to a legal nurse consultant without checking her background in cardiac care — and the matter involved a post-op MI — the resulting report read like a nursing school textbook. The defense attorney called it "medically accurate and legally useless." Three weeks and a replaced consultant later, they finally had something they could work with.

Hiring the wrong LNC doesn't just waste your retainer. It can sink a timeline, weaken your expert strategy, or hand opposing counsel an easy attack vector.

> **The Short Version:** The right legal nurse consultant has deep experience in your specific case type, produces reports attorneys can actually use in court, and communicates like a collaborator — not a clinician writing discharge instructions. Ask these 15 questions before you sign anything.

**Key Takeaways**
- Specialty match matters more than general credentials — a cardiac nurse cannot credibly evaluate orthopedic standard-of-care questions
- Sample reports are non-negotiable; credentials alone tell you nothing about analytical quality
- The best LNCs anticipate opposing counsel's arguments, not just support yours
- Communication style is a make-or-break factor that most attorneys overlook until it's too late

## The 15 Questions

**1. What is your clinical background, and how many years did you practice at the bedside?**

An LNC who spent 18 months as a floor nurse before pivoting to consulting is not the same as one with 12 years in a specialty ICU. Ask for specifics. A strong answer names a specialty, a setting (e.g., level I trauma center), and a tenure long enough to have encountered edge cases — not just textbook presentations.

**2. Do you have relevant experience in the medical specialty at the center of this case?**

General nursing knowledge has real limits when the case turns on neurosurgical protocols or neonatal resuscitation standards. A good LNC will tell you honestly when a case falls outside their wheelhouse — and that honesty is itself a green flag. If they claim expertise in everything, keep looking.

**3. What certifications do you hold, and when were they last renewed?**

The LNCC (Legal Nurse Consultant Certified) and CLNC (Certified Legal Nurse Consultant) are the two main credentials. Neither guarantees quality, but lapsed certification is a yellow flag — it suggests someone who stopped investing in the field. Ask for the renewal date, not just the credential.

**4. Can you show me two or three sample reports from comparable cases?**

This is the single most important question on this list. Read the reports. Are they written for an attorney or for a charge nurse? A good report distinguishes disputed from undisputed facts, explains medical concepts without condescension, and builds toward a clear analytical conclusion. A bad one buries you in jargon.

> **Reality Check:** Plenty of LNCs will show you their best report. Ask for a representative one — something from a case type similar to yours — and you'll see a more honest sample of their baseline work product.

**5. Can you provide references from attorneys who have retained you on similar matters?**

Peer references from attorneys — not just former supervisors or nursing colleagues — give you insight into how the LNC actually performs under the pressures of litigation. Ask the references specifically whether the LNC hit deadlines and whether the work held up during depositions or trial.

**6. How do you approach cases where the standard of care is genuinely ambiguous?**

This question separates analysts from advocates. A strong LNC can articulate where the medical evidence is unclear, where reasonable clinicians disagree, and what that ambiguity means for your case strategy. Anyone who claims every fact cuts in your favor on first review is selling, not analyzing.

**7. How familiar are you with the legal theories at issue in this type of case?**

An LNC who has worked exclusively with plaintiff's firms may have blind spots when it comes to defense strategy — and vice versa. You want someone who can anticipate the opposing argument well enough to help you prepare for it, not just someone who validates your initial theory.

**8. What does your typical timeline look like from case intake to first deliverable?**

Vague answers here are a warning sign. A well-organized LNC can tell you how long a records review and preliminary analysis takes for a case of your complexity, and what factors might push that timeline out. If they can't give you a ballpark, they may be juggling more cases than they can manage.

> **Pro Tip:** Ask how many active matters they're currently handling. A one-person operation with 20 open files is a different risk profile than a two-person shop with eight.

**9. How do you handle tight or shifting court deadlines?**

Litigation doesn't care about your LNC's vacation schedule. Ask for a specific example of a case where a deadline compressed unexpectedly and how they responded. "I've never had that happen" is not a reassuring answer.

**10. What does your deliverable package typically include?**

Some LNCs deliver a narrative report. Others include a chronological medical summary, a deviation-from-standard analysis, a list of potential expert witness candidates, and a list of suggested discovery questions — all as separate documents. Know what you're getting before you negotiate the fee.

**11. Have you ever served as a testifying expert witness?**

If there's any chance you'll need your LNC to step from behind the scenes into a deposition or courtroom, this question is essential. Not all LNCs are comfortable in that role, and testifying experience carries a different preparation burden. Get clarity on this upfront.

**12. How do you handle situations where your analysis doesn't support the client's theory?**

The honest answer is some version of: "I tell the attorney clearly and early, so they can adjust their strategy." An LNC who hedges this question — or who implies they'd soften the finding — is someone who will eventually cost you more than they save.

**13. How do you stay current with clinical practice standards in your specialty?**

Standard of care evolves. An LNC relying on protocols from five years ago may inadvertently anchor your case to an outdated benchmark. Ask about continuing education, professional memberships (e.g., AALNC), and recent cases where they had to research a changing clinical standard.

**14. What is your fee structure, and what triggers additional billing?**

Hourly rates, flat-fee projects, and hybrid arrangements all have different risk profiles for your matter. More importantly, ask what scope changes trigger a new invoice — additional records, a second set of interrogatories, a deposition prep session. Surprises here are expensive.

| Fee Model | Best For | Watch Out For |
|---|---|---|
| Hourly | Open-ended or complex cases | Scope creep with no ceiling |
| Flat-fee per phase | Defined deliverables (records review, report) | Change orders for anything outside scope |
| Hybrid | Long-running litigation with defined milestones | Ambiguous milestone definitions |

**15. Have you worked on cases that went to trial, and what was your role?**

Trial experience sharpens an LNC's understanding of what actually matters — what a jury can process, what a judge will sustain, what an expert can credibly defend on the stand. It's not disqualifying if they haven't, but it's genuinely valuable if they have.

## Practical Bottom Line

Print this list. Use it as a phone screen before you commit to a formal engagement. Any LNC worth retaining will take the questions seriously — and many will appreciate that you're approaching the relationship like a professional.

Three questions that separate the top 10% from the rest: *Can I see sample reports? What does your fee structure include? And what do you do when the evidence doesn't support my theory?* If those three land well, the rest of the conversation usually confirms it.
